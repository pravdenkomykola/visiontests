What is the Tumbling E eye chart test?

A tumbling E chart is a vision chart that only uses E’s. As with a ‘standard’ eye chart, they get smaller as they go down the chart but they also rotate direction.tumbling E chart is used to measure visual acuity, especially when a person cannot read letters, such as young children or individuals who speak different languages.

Understanding Your Vision Test Results, How to Read Your Score

This vision chart uses a 2/10 scale format, which works at arm’s length (approximately 2 feet) instead of the traditional 20-foot distance used in eye doctor offices.The format is: Your Distance / Normal Vision Distance
For example:
1)2/10 means you must stand 2 feet away to see what a person with normal vision can see from 10 feet away
2)2/2 means you see at 2 feet what a person with normal vision sees at 2 feet
3)2/1.5 means you see at 2 feet what a person with normal vision would need to move closer (to 1.5 feet) to see
Your vision quality categories look reasonable:
2/1.5 = Perfect/excellent (20/15 equivalent)
2/2 = Good/normal (20/20 equivalent)
2/3 = Acceptable (20/30 equivalent)
2/4 to 2/6 = Poor (20/40 to 20/60)
2/8 to 2/10 = Very poor (20/80 to 20/100)

What is Miopia?

Nearsightedness (myopia) is when close-up objects look clear but distant objects are blurry. For instance, you can read a map clearly but have trouble seeing well enough to drive a car. Nearsightedness is a common eye focusing disorder. It has been on the rise for several decades. It is estimated that by 2050, nearly half the people in the world will have nearsightedness.

What Is Hyperopia?

Farsightedness, also called hyperopia, is a refractive error. This is when the eye does not refract—or bend—light properly. A farsighted person may not notice any problems with their vision, especially when they are young.Symptoms of farsightedness is a difficulty with close tasks like reading, eye crossing in young children, squinting. A person's age and severity of their farsightedness impacts how it affects their vision. Near vision is blurry while distance vision is generally clearer.

What is an astigmatism?

To understand astigmatism, it is helpful to think of the normal eye as evenly rounded, like a basketball. With astigmatism, the eye is egg- or oval-shaped like a North American football.
There are two basic types of astigmatism:
horizontal astigmatism (an eye wider than it is tall)
vertical astigmatism (an eye taller than it is wide)
With either type of astigmatism, near and far vision is blurry because of the eye’s irregular shape.
